Finding 1 — Scope dominates on Sonnet and Haiku from T1

Every Scope combination (H1, M1, M2, M3) failed at T1 on Sonnet and failed frequently on Haiku. The grounding rule — "use only figures explicitly stated in the data" — was violated before any other rule was added. Adding Chained or Situation rules alongside Scope did not change when failure started. Scope is the ceiling, not the combination. Opus held Scope combinations through T5–T6 with isolated single-tier failures only.

Finding 2 — Chained + Situation compounds on Haiku (M4)

M4 (Chained + Situation, no Scope) failed T3–T7 on Haiku — five consecutive tiers. In Round 1, neither Chained nor Situation alone produced this kind of sustained collapse on Haiku. Together they do. This is the clearest compounding signal in the data: two conditional logic categories stacked together exceed what Haiku can track simultaneously. Sonnet had only a single T2 failure on M4. Opus had a single T5 failure.

Finding 3 — Opus is resilient but not immune

Opus had isolated single-tier failures across H1, M1, M2, M4, E1, E2, E3 but recovered in the following tier every time. No consecutive collapse occurred. This means Opus can support complex multi-rule system prompts with a low failure rate — but critical rules still need redundancy because isolated failures do occur even at low tier counts.

Finding 4 — Control tests failed on Haiku (warning sign)

E1, E2, and E3 are low or zero weight categories that should pass all seven tiers. Haiku failed E1 at T1, T2, T6, T7 and E2 at T3, T4, T5, T7. This means Haiku is not just struggling with hard categories — it is inconsistent at a basic level with simple rule types. This limits how much you can trust Haiku even for straightforward structured tasks at rule counts above 2.

Finding 5 — Scope did not clearly compound with other categories

The hypothesis was that combining Scope with Chained or Situation would produce earlier failures than Scope alone. The data does not clearly support this. On Sonnet, Scope failed at T1 both alone (Round 1) and in every cross-category combination. The combinations did not make things worse — Scope was already the dominant failure driver regardless of what was paired with it. This is itself a meaningful finding: for grounding-heavy prompts, the model choice matters more than the combination design.
